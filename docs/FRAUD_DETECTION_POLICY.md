## 1. Introduction

This Fraud Detection and Monitoring Policy (**Policy**) is adopted by **Sankalp Insurance Brokers Private Limited** (**Company**), operating the **InsureIT** Platform, to establish a structured framework for preventing, detecting, reporting, investigating, monitoring, and mitigating fraud risks relating to insurance e-commerce, digital insurance assistance, commercial vehicle insurance support, claim assistance, customer onboarding, policy servicing, document handling, payment workflows, and cyber-enabled activities.

The Company recognizes that fraud can harm customers, insurers, intermediaries, employees, business partners, regulators, and the integrity of the insurance ecosystem. The Company is committed to maintaining high standards of ethics, compliance, transparency, information security, customer protection, and responsible business conduct.

This Policy is intended to align with applicable laws, rules, regulations, and regulatory guidance relating to insurance intermediaries, insurance e-commerce, fraud monitoring, information security, data protection, cyber security, anti-money laundering, and customer due diligence, as amended from time to time.

## 2. Objective of the Policy

The objectives of this Policy are to:

- establish an effective fraud monitoring and control framework;
- define fraud, cyber fraud, internal fraud, third-party fraud, and claim-related fraud;
- create awareness among employees, customers, partners, vendors, and other stakeholders;
- prevent fraudulent activities through appropriate controls and governance;
- detect actual, suspected, attempted, or potential fraud at an early stage;
- provide a clear reporting mechanism for fraud concerns;
- support fair and objective investigation of fraud cases;
- ensure appropriate reporting to insurers, regulators, law enforcement, or other authorities where required;
- reduce financial, operational, legal, regulatory, reputational, and customer harm;
- maintain proper records, audit trails, and evidence for investigation and compliance;
- promote ethical conduct and zero tolerance towards fraud.

## 4. Definition of Fraud

For the purpose of this Policy, **fraud** means any act, omission, concealment, misrepresentation, abuse of authority, breach of trust, or dishonest or unlawful conduct intended to obtain an unfair advantage, cause wrongful gain, avoid liability, cause wrongful loss, manipulate a process, misuse data, or influence an insurance, financial, operational, or claim-related decision.

Fraud may include, but is not limited to:

- misappropriation of funds;
- deliberate misstatement or concealment of facts;
- non-disclosure of material information;
- use of forged, fake, altered, or fabricated documents;
- abuse of responsibility, authority, access, or fiduciary position;
- manipulation of customer, vehicle, policy, claim, or payment records;
- cyber-enabled fraud;
- unauthorized access, extraction, sharing, alteration, or deletion of information;
- false claims or inflated claims;
- collusion between employees, customers, vendors, service providers, or third parties;
- issuing or representing fake insurance policies;
- collecting premium or payments in an unauthorized manner;
- using stolen payment instruments or bank details;
- impersonation of the Company, insurer, customer, employee, surveyor, or service provider.

## 5. Cyber or New-Age Fraud

**Cyber or new-age fraud** means any fraud carried out using digital platforms, electronic systems, internet services, mobile applications, APIs, cloud systems, messaging platforms, payment systems, social engineering, malware, phishing, or other technology-enabled methods.

Examples may include:

- phishing or fake communication in the name of the Company or InsureIT;
- fake websites, fake apps, or fake social media pages;
- unauthorized access to user accounts;
- credential theft;
- misuse of OTPs, passwords, or authentication tokens;
- malware or ransomware attacks;
- email account compromise;
- fake payment links;
- manipulation of digital claim records;
- unauthorized data scraping;
- fraudulent upload of documents or images;
- use of stolen cards, bank accounts, UPI IDs, or payment credentials;
- compromise of customer, policy, claim, or document data.

## 6. Categories of Fraud

### 6.1 Internal Fraud

Internal fraud includes fraudulent acts committed by directors, officers, employees, contractual staff, field executives, claim processors, managers, administrators, or any person acting on behalf of the Company.

Examples include:

- misuse of official access;
- unauthorized alteration of records;
- manipulation of claim, policy, or customer data;
- misappropriation of funds;
- accepting unauthorized benefits;
- creation of fake records;
- unauthorized sharing of customer data;
- collusion with customers, vendors, garages, surveyors, or third parties;
- issuing or facilitating fake policy documents;
- misuse of Company assets, systems, documents, or credentials.

### 6.2 Third-Party or External Fraud

Third-party fraud includes fraud committed by persons or entities outside the Company, including customers, claimants, vehicle owners, drivers, vendors, garages, surveyors, repairers, payment users, cyber attackers, or other external parties.

Examples include:

- false claim submission;
- forged or manipulated documents;
- fake accident reporting;
- inflated repair bills;
- false ownership or vehicle details;
- fake identity or KYC documents;
- stolen payment credentials;
- unauthorized access to accounts;
- impersonation of Company staff or insurer representatives.

### 6.3 Affinity, Collusive, or Complex Fraud

Affinity or complex fraud includes fraud involving collusion between multiple persons or entities, such as employees, customers, garages, vendors, surveyors, intermediaries, or external fraudsters.

Examples include:

- collusion to submit false claims;
- inflated repair estimates with shared benefits;
- repeated claims involving the same parties;
- use of fake documents across multiple cases;
- organized activity to manipulate insurance or claim workflows;
- coordinated misuse of customer data or payment systems.

### 6.4 Policyholder, Customer, or Claims Fraud

Policyholder or claims fraud includes fraudulent activity by any person seeking coverage, policy servicing, claim settlement, refund, payment, or other insurance-related benefit.

Examples include:

- misrepresentation in quote or proposal information;
- non-disclosure of material facts;
- false declaration of accident facts;
- inflated or fictitious loss;
- forged repair bills or invoices;
- false driver details;
- fabricated vehicle damage;
- repeated claims for the same loss;
- submission of altered policy or claim documents.

## 7. Fraud Management and Governance Structure

The Company may constitute a **Fraud Management and Governance Team** (**FMGT**) to review, recommend, monitor, and support the fraud prevention, detection, reporting, and investigation framework.

The FMGT may include the following representatives or such other members as the Company may designate:

1. IT / Information Security SPOC;
2. Legal and Compliance SPOC;
3. Business and Operations SPOC;
4. Claims / Customer Support SPOC, where required;
5. Any other departmental SPOC invited on a need basis.

The primary administration of the FMGT may rest with the Principal Officer, Chief Executive Officer, Compliance Officer, or any person designated by the Company, in coordination with relevant departmental heads and business functions.

## 8. Responsibilities of FMGT

The FMGT may be responsible for:

- recommending fraud prevention and detection processes;
- reviewing fraud risk indicators and control gaps;
- supporting investigation of actual or suspected fraud;
- documenting investigation findings and recommendations;
- recommending corrective, disciplinary, legal, or operational actions;
- coordinating with departments for information and evidence;
- recommending reporting to insurers, regulators, law enforcement, or other authorities where required;
- maintaining or recommending fraud case records and red flag databases;
- coordinating awareness initiatives for employees and relevant stakeholders;
- periodically reviewing this Policy and fraud control measures;
- recommending system, process, and access control improvements.

## 9. Duties of Employees, Staff, and Authorized Users

All employees, staff, contractors, and authorized users must:

- act honestly, ethically, and in good faith;
- comply with Company policies, laws, and applicable regulations;
- avoid involvement in any fraudulent activity;
- protect customer, policy, claim, payment, and Company information;
- use system access only for authorized business purposes;
- report actual, suspected, attempted, or potential fraud promptly;
- cooperate with internal reviews and investigations;
- preserve relevant records, documents, logs, messages, and evidence;
- not retaliate against whistleblowers or persons reporting concerns in good faith;
- not destroy, alter, hide, or manipulate evidence.

## 11. Whistleblower and Protected Disclosure

A person may submit a protected disclosure regarding actual or suspected fraud. The Company may allow anonymous reporting, subject to available reporting channels.

Protected disclosures should be made as soon as possible after becoming aware of the issue and should contain specific details to allow effective review and investigation.

The Company will make reasonable efforts to keep the identity of a whistleblower confidential, subject to legal, regulatory, investigation, disciplinary, or law enforcement requirements.

No person reporting a concern in good faith should be subjected to retaliation, harassment, victimization, or adverse action merely for making such a report.

## 12. Preliminary Review and Investigation

Upon receiving a fraud report, the FMGT or designated team may conduct a preliminary review to determine whether the matter requires investigation.

If the preliminary review indicates that the concern has no reasonable basis, is outside the scope of this Policy, or does not require further action, the matter may be closed with appropriate documentation.

If further investigation is required, the FMGT or a designated investigator may conduct a fair, objective, and neutral fact-finding investigation. The investigation may include:

- review of system logs and audit trails;
- review of documents, claims, policies, records, or payment data;
- interviews with relevant persons;
- coordination with insurers, service providers, vendors, or departments;
- preservation of evidence;
- review of access logs, device logs, emails, communications, and uploaded files;
- assessment of financial, operational, legal, customer, and security impact.

The investigation does not presume guilt and should be conducted fairly and confidentially to the extent possible.

## 13. Action After Investigation

Based on investigation findings, the Company may take appropriate action, including:

- closure of the matter where no fraud is found;
- process correction or control improvement;
- account suspension or access restriction;
- rejection or escalation of suspicious documents or claims;
- reporting to the relevant insurer;
- disciplinary action against employees or staff;
- recovery of loss or damages;
- termination of contract or vendor relationship;
- legal action;
- reporting to regulators, law enforcement, cyber authorities, or other competent authorities;
- customer notification where required by law;
- strengthening security, compliance, or operational controls.

## 14. Reporting to Insurers, Regulators, and Law Enforcement

On a case-to-case basis, where the Company reasonably believes that fraud has been committed or attempted, it may report the matter to:

- the relevant insurance company;
- law enforcement authorities;
- cybercrime authorities;
- regulators;
- courts or competent authorities;
- payment gateways, banks, or financial institutions;
- other market participants or service providers where permitted by law.

The Company may cooperate with such authorities or entities for investigation, prevention, mitigation, recovery, and disposal of fraud cases.

## 15. Confidentiality and Data Protection

Fraud reports, investigation records, evidence, whistleblower details, customer information, policy data, claim data, payment data, and employee information must be handled confidentially and only shared on a need-to-know basis.

All handling of personal information must be consistent with the Company’s Privacy Policy, applicable data protection laws, contractual obligations, and legal requirements.

## 16. Record Keeping

The Company may maintain records of fraud reports, investigation findings, evidence, decisions, actions taken, and reporting made to insurers or authorities. Such records may be retained for legal, regulatory, audit, compliance, operational, or dispute resolution purposes.

## 17. Awareness and Training

## 19. Annexure A: Red Flag Indicators

The following red flag indicators are illustrative and not exhaustive.

### 19.1 Customer, Policy, or Proposal Red Flags

- misrepresentation of facts in proposal, quote, policy, claim, or other documents;
- non-disclosure of material facts;
- inconsistent customer, vehicle, driver, policy, or ownership details;
- repeated changes in contact details, bank details, or nominee details;
- unusual urgency or pressure to process without verification;
- documents that appear altered, forged, blurred, cropped, or inconsistent;
- mismatch between RC, policy, vehicle number, chassis number, engine number, or owner name;
- suspicious payment source or third-party payment without explanation;
- repeated quote or policy requests using similar details across different identities.

### 19.2 Claim Red Flags

- wrong or inconsistent information regarding claim facts;
- wrong or unclear cause of loss or damage;
- claim for fictitious, inflated, repeated, or unrelated damages;
- accident details inconsistent with photographs, survey findings, repair estimate, or vehicle condition;
- suspicious delay in reporting accident;
- repeated claims involving the same vehicle, driver, garage, location, or contact number;
- forged or inflated repair bills, estimates, invoices, or receipts;
- mismatched date, time, location, GPS, photograph metadata, or damage pattern;
- multiple claims for the same accident or damage;
- attempt to influence staff, surveyor, garage, or processor.

### 19.3 Cyber and E-Commerce Red Flags

- illegal hacking, attempted hacking, or unauthorized system access;
- compromised email, account, or admin credentials;
- website, app, or portal intrusion or defacement;
- phishing, malware, fake links, or fake communication in the name of the Company or InsureIT;
- suspicious login attempts, unusual IPs, repeated failed logins, or abnormal access patterns;
- fake or stolen card, UPI, bank, or payment details;
- bogus online activity intended to generate illegitimate benefit or revenue;
- unauthorized scraping, extraction, or sharing of customer data;
- manipulation of system records, workflows, claim status, or documents;
- unauthorized upload, deletion, or alteration of documents;
- suspicious API usage or automated abuse.

### 19.4 Employee, Vendor, or Internal Red Flags

- employee involvement in fraudulent policies, claims, or records;
- forging or altering customer details in proposal forms, policy documents, claim records, or other documents;
- unauthorized sharing, manipulation, or theft of customer data;
- unauthorized acceptance of money, gifts, rebates, or benefits;
- issuing fake documents in the name of insurers or the Company;
- misappropriation of funds or premium amounts;
- unauthorized access outside job responsibility;
- deletion or alteration of audit logs, documents, or evidence;
- theft or misuse of Company assets such as laptops, phones, documents, credentials, or access cards;
- collusion with garages, vendors, customers, surveyors, or third parties.
